Blog 8

Digital Inequalities

by Zandile Gebuza on 22 April 2024

Close reading of Making a Feminist Internet in Africa by Sheena Magenya

Sheena is a feminist with over a decade years of civil society working experience in Namibia, South Africa and Kenya. She has a background in media and communications, and is interested in the opportunities that African women have to influence and effect change in social and political spaces, including online spaces. She is curious about technology and the internet, and passionate about an intersectional feminist approach to social justice. She holds a BA in Media Studies and Psychology from the University of Namibia, and a Masters in Creative Writing from the University of the Witwatersrand.
(Association for Progressive Communications, 2020)


Sheena Magenya's article discusses the importance of African feminists and their involvement in shaping the internet to make it more inclusive and representative. She wrote this during the COVID-19 pandemic, where there was an increased reliance on the internet for communication and information. The internet and other communication technologies became a lifeline because they were presented as a solution to a social problem (Magenya, 2020, p.2). However, this solution disregarded the inequality that exists in access to the internet and the fact that the most disadvantaged people during this time were African women and queer African people.

"The inequality of access to and representation on the internet is fast replicating existing oppression and suppression. Africa’s particular voice and history in this dynamic needs to be a loud and powerful narrative that shifts the dominant white tide towards a different and more inclusive internet" (p.4).

In the article, Magenya talks about gatherings that happened aiming to create a feminist internet, with a focus on the African context. These gatherings brought together activists, thinkers, artists, and feminists to discuss the impact of technology and the internet on their lives and work. They also aimed to challenge the idea that technology automatically solves social problems and emphasize the importance of diverse voices in shaping online spaces. One key point that was raised across all gatherings is the issue of online gender-based violence, which disproportionately affects women, the LGBTQIA+ community, and gender non-conforming people. Magenya even states that the worst part is that even though the use of the internet in Africa is increasing, there is little to no legislation to address this violence in many countries (Magenya, 2020, p.3). That is why she believes that the internet should not replicate existing inequalities and oppressions but should instead be a space for empowerment and inclusion.

"...we need more African feminists taking up space, telling stories, giving alternative views, creating body positive content, showing off our bodies and talents and skills. African feminists make more room for African women online" (p.6).

Further on in the article, Magenya addresses the perception of Africa as a passive recipient of technology rather than an innovator. When it comes to technology, Africa is often positioned as a beneficiary, not an innovator of it (Magenya, 2020, p.5). She challenges this narrative by highlighting the contributions of African women, LGBTQIA individuals, and people living with disabilities in shaping the internet and using technology to improve their lives. That is why the article emphasizes the need for more African feminists to take up space online. They need to tell their stories, and challenge dominant narratives. It also relates to something I learnt from the The World in WWW/Graham et al. lecture video, If our stories are not told and if we don't shape the narratives about ourselves, we become invisible or are discounted (Geyser, H. 2020).

In conclusion, this reading taught me that it is important for the internet to be feminist and have more African voices. This is essential for promoting diversity, safety, and fun online. It also taught me that it is not only necessary but crucial, especially when global crises like COVID-19 pandemic happen.

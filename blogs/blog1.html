Imagining the Internet

by Zandile Gebuza on 15 February 2024

From this week's reading list, I chose to read: What Hypertext Is by Noah Wardrip-Fruin and As We May Think At 65 by Simon Harper. I chose both of these readings because they are much shorter than the others and I figured it would be easier for me to engage with two short readings instead of one long one.

The Wardrip-Fruin reading taught me a lot. Before reading it, I didn't know what "hypertext" is and after reading it, I still don't know what it is ðŸ˜…, but I kind of understand it. In the article, the author explores the concept of hypertext by breaking down some definitions that were provided by different academics. He then mentions that to truly understand the concept, one should analyse the definition given by the thinker who coined [it] (Wardrip-Fruin, p.126). Theodore Holm Nelson, the creator of hypertext, defines it as forms of writing which branch or perform on request [and] are best presented on computer screens (Wardrip-Fruin, p.127). After engaging with this reading, I attempted to use what I learnt to create my own definition. Hyper- means excessive and text is used to decsribe letters/words/forms of writing, so put together, I define hypertext as something that gives a person access to lots of words. To relate this to my experience of the internet an example of hypertext would be Wikipedia. This is because when I search for something on the site, all the articles have links or references to other pages with similar content. Therefore, Wikipedia is hypertext because it gives me access to lots of other information.

The Harper reading was a bit harder to understand. This is because I haven't read the essay by Vannevar Bush, that it is based on, however, I was still able to learn a few things from it. This reading outlines the impact that As We May Think has made, 65 years later. Harper tells the reader about how Bush created the MEMEX. This is a device which stores an individual's books, records, and communications. It is also mechanised so that it may be consulted with exceeding speed and flexibility (Harper, p.2). The author also mentions that MEMEX is credited as being inspiration for the modern Web. He also states that Bush wanted computers to help users with whatever they needed and for information to be readily available to them. To relate this to my experience of the internet, this idea of MEMEX basically describes what browsers are. I can search for something on the internet and I instantly get referred to websites that match my request which are organised by relevance and popularity.

After reading the aforementioned articles, I realised that I know a lot about computers, websites, and the internet but I don't really know about how they came to be. Therefore, I did some quick Google searches for some things that came to mind. Below are some of the things I learnt:

  • I learnt that the web was first created as a way for scientists to share experiment data with each other but it grew to become more than that.
  • I learnt that Tim Berners-Lee created the first website in 1991 and he also coined the term "World Wide Web".
  • Also, I learnt that the internet and the web are not the same thing. This was shocking to me. I always thought that they are the same or that they are interchangable words to describe the same term but it turns out that they are not. The next quote helped me better understand the difference between the two. The internet is a huge network of computers all over the world, which are connected together whereas the world wide web is a collection of web pages found on this network of computers (CBBC Newsround, 2019).
